### 9. Shock and Lead (Attention-Then-Explanation Story)
**Description**: This narrative structure opens with a **single, striking fact or data point** designed to capture attention and create urgency. The audience is immediately confronted with a surprising, alarming, or counterintuitive finding. The story then **leads** into a set of visuals that explain the drivers, context, or factors behind that shock—turning the initial reaction into understanding. This approach is highly effective for making a memorable impact and then grounding it in evidence, avoiding sensationalism without explanation.

**Trend Example: "Employee Turnover Reached 47% in Our Engineering Division Last Year"**
* **Figure 1 (The Shock Fact):** A single KPI or headline chart displays the striking statistic—e.g., "47% Annual Turnover" or a bar comparing the division to industry benchmark (15%)—immediately establishing urgency and surprise.
* **Figure 2 (Explanatory Factors):** Subsequent visuals break down the drivers: a stacked bar or treemap showing turnover by tenure band (e.g., highest among 0–2 year employees), a heat map of exit reasons (compensation, growth, manager), or a timeline of policy changes. Together they explain *why* the shock occurred and set up actionable insights.
